\begin{column}{\colwidth}

  \begin{block}{Motivation}
    We built a \textbf{Quantum Natural Language Processing (QNLP)} pipeline that
    classifies sentence sentiment as \textbf{\color{poscol}positive} or
    \textbf{\color{negcol}negative} using a parameterized quantum circuit.
    Grammar determines the circuit structure; each word contributes a learned rotation
    angle. The final measurement collapses the state to a class label.
    We train and evaluate on sentence pairs such as
    \wordbox{poscol}{the clever program runs} vs.\
    \wordbox{negcol}{the broken code fails}.
    \smallskip
    \begin{center}\normalsize
      \textbf{\color{poscol}positive} $\equiv |1\rangle$ \quad$\mid$\quad
      \textbf{\color{negcol}negative} $\equiv |0\rangle$
    \end{center}
  \end{block}

  \begin{alertblock}{End-to-End Architecture}
    \begin{center}\large
      \textbf{words $\to$ rotation parameters} \;$\cdot$\;
      \textbf{grammar $\to$ circuit structure \& weights}
      \;$\cdot$\; \textbf{circuit $\to$ measure $\to$ class label}
    \end{center}
    Each word is represented by a single trainable rotation angle.
    Grammar determines how those angles are composed into a circuit.
    Measuring the output qubit yields $P(|1\rangle)$, interpreted as the
    probability of \textbf{\color{poscol}positive} sentiment.
  \end{alertblock}

  \begin{block}{Step 1 --- Grammatical roles and learned weights}
    All sentences share a common syntactic structure; each word is assigned
    a colored \textbf{grammatical role}:
    \begin{center}\large
      \wordbox{adjcol}{clever}\;\wordbox{nouncol}{program}\;\wordbox{verbcol}{runs}
      \;\wordbox{objcol}{(the puzzle)}
    \end{center}
    \begin{center}\footnotesize
      {\color{adjcol!60!black}\textbf{adjective}}\quad
      {\color{nouncol!60!black}\textbf{subject}}\quad
      {\color{verbcol!60!black}\textbf{verb}}\quad
      {\color{objcol!60!black}\textbf{object}}
    \end{center}

    Grammar wires words together and weights each role's contribution.
    Verbs and adjectives carry more weight, since they drive sentiment:

    \begin{center}
      \begin{tabular}{lc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{role} & \textbf{weight} \\
        \midrule
        {\color{verbcol!60!black}verb}        & \textbf{1.40} \\
        {\color{adjcol!60!black}adjective}    & \textbf{1.25} \\
        object-adjective                      & 0.90 \\
        {\color{nouncol!60!black}subject}     & 0.45 \\
        {\color{objcol!60!black}object}       & 0.35 \\
        \bottomrule
      \end{tabular}
    \end{center}
    \footnotesize (Object-role weights only apply when the sentence has an explicit object;
    ``the clever program runs'' uses only the top three.)
  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Step 2 --- Qubit states and measurement}
    A classical bit takes a definite value in $\{0,1\}$. A \textbf{qubit} is a
    two-level quantum system whose pure state is a unit vector on the
    \textbf{Bloch sphere}. Before measurement, the qubit exists in a
    \textbf{superposition} of $|0\rangle$ and $|1\rangle$.

    \begin{itemize}
      \item An $R_y(\theta)$ rotation from $|0\rangle$ yields
            $|\psi\rangle = \cos(\theta/2)|0\rangle + \sin(\theta/2)|1\rangle$,
            a point at polar angle $\theta$ from the north pole of the Bloch sphere.
      \item \textbf{Measurement} in the $\{|0\rangle,|1\rangle\}$ basis projects
            $|\psi\rangle$ onto one of the basis states. The probability of each
            outcome equals the \textbf{squared magnitude of the projection}:
            $P(|1\rangle)=|\langle 1|\psi\rangle|^2=\sin^2(\theta/2)$.
            The equatorial state ($\theta=\pi/2$) gives $P(|0\rangle)=P(|1\rangle)=\tfrac{1}{2}$.
    \end{itemize}
    We use a \textbf{2-qubit register}: a \textbf{\color{nouncol}subject qubit} and a
    \textbf{\color{verbcol}sentence qubit} whose measurement yields the classification.
    The joint state lives in the tensor product space
    $\mathcal{H}_{\mathrm{subj}} \otimes \mathcal{H}_{\mathrm{sent}}$;
    amplitudes of product states multiply.
  \end{block}

\end{column}

  \begin{block}{Step 3 --- Encoding words as rotation angles}
    Each word maps to an $R_y(\theta)$ rotation on its qubit wire.
    Grammar dictates which angles go on which wire.
    For \wordbox{poscol}{the clever program runs}, the \emph{trained} parameters are
    \;{\color{adjcol!60!black}\textbf{clever}}$=0.098$,\;
    {\color{nouncol!60!black}\textbf{program}}$=0.043$,\;
    {\color{verbcol!60!black}\textbf{runs}}$=0.489$:

    \medskip
    \begin{center}\large
    \begin{tabular}{r l}
      {\color{nouncol!60!black}\textbf{subject $\theta$}}  & $=$ clever $+$ program $= 0.098+0.043 = \mathbf{0.141}$\\[4pt]
      {\color{verbcol!60!black}\textbf{sentence $\theta$}} & $=$ runs $= \mathbf{0.489}$\\[4pt]
      \textbf{verdict angle} & $=\ \underbrace{\pi/2}_{\text{equatorial init.}}+\ \underbrace{\text{score}}_{\text{weighted sum}}$\\[10pt]
      \multicolumn{2}{r}{$\text{score}=\underbrace{0.068}_{\text{bias}}+1.25(0.098)+0.45(0.043)+1.40(0.489)=0.894$}\\[6pt]
      \multicolumn{2}{r}{verdict angle $=\pi/2+0.894=\mathbf{2.465}$}
    \end{tabular}
    \end{center}
    \smallskip
    The sentence qubit starts at the equatorial state ($\theta=\pi/2$),
    so the weighted score tips it toward either pole --- the verb
    dominates (weight 1.40).

    \footnotesize An $R_y(\theta)$ gate rotates the qubit state vector by angle $\theta$

  \begin{alertblock}{Step 4 --- Entanglement via the CNOT Gate}
    Grammar requires the \textbf{\color{nouncol}subject} and
    \textbf{\color{verbcol}sentence} qubits to be treated as a single composite system.
    The \textbf{CNOT (CX) gate} encodes this grammatical composition:

    \begin{center}\large
      \textbf{If subject $=|1\rangle$: flip sentence qubit.\quad
      If subject $=|0\rangle$: leave sentence qubit unchanged.}
    \end{center}

    \heading{Action on the computational basis}
    The 2-qubit system has four computational basis states (left digit $=$ subject,
    right $=$ sentence). CNOT \textbf{swaps the two states where the subject is $|1\rangle$}
    ($|10\rangle\leftrightarrow|11\rangle$):
    \begin{center}\large
      \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
        \toprule
         & \textbf{00} & \textbf{01} & \cellcolor{hi!40}\textbf{10} & \cellcolor{hi!40}\textbf{11}\\
        \midrule
        before & 0.968 & 0.241 & \cellcolor{hi!40}0.068 & \cellcolor{hi!40}0.017\\
        after  & 0.968 & 0.241 & \cellcolor{hi!40}0.017 & \cellcolor{hi!40}0.068\\
        \bottomrule
      \end{tabular}
    \end{center}

    Because each qubit is in superposition, the CNOT produces an
    \textbf{entangled} state: the result can no longer be written as a
    tensor product $|\psi_{\mathrm{subj}}\rangle\otimes|\psi_{\mathrm{sent}}\rangle$
    of the two qubits separately.
  \end{alertblock}

    The 2-qubit state is tracked as four computational basis amplitudes

    \textbf{left digit $=$ \color{nouncol}subject}, \textbf{right $=$

        \bottomrule

    \end{center}
    The sentence qubit's word angle ($\theta=0.489$) leaves it near the $|0\rangle$ pole;

      $P(|1\rangle) = 0.993^2 + 0.039^2 = \mathbf{0.988}

  \begin{alertblock}{Training: the parameter-shift rule}
    Error: $e = P(|1\rangle) - y_{\mathrm{label}}$. Gradients are computed exactly
    by re-running the circuit at $\theta\pm\tfrac{\pi}{2}$:
    \vspace{-0.5em}
    \begin{align*}
      \frac{\partial P}{\partial\theta} &=
        \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left[\,P\!\left(\theta{+}\tfrac{\pi}{2}\right)
        - P\!\left(\theta{-}\tfrac{\pi}{2}\right)\,\right], \qquad
      \theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta\!\left(e\cdot\frac{\partial P}{\partial\theta}\right)
    \end{align*}
    \vspace{-1em}

    This identity holds exactly for gates $e^{-i\theta G/2}$, making it executable
    on real quantum hardware. Training proceeds for $\sim\!50$ epochs.
  \end{alertblock}
